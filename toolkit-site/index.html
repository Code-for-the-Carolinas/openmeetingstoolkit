STEEPLE Analysis

PEST analysis comes from Harvard Business Professor Francis J. Aguilar's 1964 book Scanning the Business Environment. There are many variations on the model. This version, from management training firm Professional Academy, seems well suited to our purpose of an environmental scan for evaluating the openness of public meetings. The letters have been rearranged to STEEPLE so it is easier to remember and also organizes some related concepts in a more logical order (example: political next to legal).

Social

What are some of the demographic characteristics of your community?

Are there any current social trends or controversies likely to be the subject of public meetings?

Technical

How do people in your community access the internet?

Is public transportation available in your community?

What types of technology does your local government use in public meetings?

What is the condition of local government websites relevant to public meetings?

Ethical

What factors will be important to center marginalized voices in your community's public meetings?

Have public officials or community members faced violence or threats related to public meetings?

How will you build a representative and inclusive Open Meetings project team?

Economic

What is the income of a typical local household?

Are there areas of concentrated poverty in your community?

What is your local government's budget?

What are some of the main employers in your community?

Political

Who are the relevant local elected officials?

Who is the mayor of your city?

Who are the county commissioners?

Are there other relevant officials (city manager, sheriff)?

What are key dates in the next election cycle?

Are there any other local groups active in advocating around open meetings?

Legal

Public Meetings in North Carolina are governed by Article 33C, Meetings of Public Bodies. “Open Meetings and Other Legal Requirements for Local Government Boards” by Frayda S. Bluestein at the UNC School of Government is a useful resource to understand the legal context of public meetings in North Carolina.

Are there any additional laws related to public meetings in your city or county? These laws would typically be found in the Code of Ordinances or in the bylaws for an individual board or commission.

How could you address a situation in which the current policy is not being followed in practice?

Through what avenues could you propose changes to current policy?

Environmental

What weather conditions or geographic features make it challenging to access public meetings on site?

Meeting Evaluation

Our meeting evaluation tools will be based on the following findings by Code for Asheville, Accessibility Beyond Compliance, as well as additional sources on usability:

During the creation of this policy proposal, the following challenges with public participation were identified:

  • The current public comment process requires a burdensome advance sign-up process that requires disclosure of unnecessary personal information.
  • Meeting materials do not contain a clear indication of updates or modifications to documents that were posted previously.
  • Local government entities lack data-driven metrics of public participation, such as the number of comments or the methods under which they were provided. As a result, it is challenging for staff, elected officials, or the public to analyze how changes to the public meeting process increase or decrease public participation.
  • Due to a lack of consistency in the process for public participation, it can be difficult to evaluate public support or opposition to measures considered by public bodies.

Before the Meeting

All Meetings

  1. What is the name of the public body holding the meeting? A public body could be a board, commission, council, or other group.
  2. What is the date of the meeting?
  3. A meeting agenda lists the topics to be discussed during a meeting. When was the meeting agenda available (allow only one selection):
    • At least one week in advance (7+ days before the meeting)
    • 1-6 days before the meeting
    • The day of the meeting
    • It was never available
  4. What logistical/practical information are you able to find (mark all that apply):
    • Meeting location/address
    • Meeting time
    • Parking information
    • Public transit information
    • Accessibility considerations that will be provided
    • Instructions on requesting additional accessibility measures
    • None of the above
  5. Before attending the meeting, which of the following, if any, were you able to locate? In this question, the word “board” refers to the board, commission, council, or other group holding the meeting (mark all that apply):
    • Board membership
    • Board contact info
    • Board meeting schedules
    • Board bylaws
    • Last time website was updated
    • None of the above
  6. Can you submit questions by voicemail or email before the meeting?
    • Yes, by voicemail only
    • Yes, by email only
    • Yes, by both email and voicemail
    • No
    • Unsure
    • You must submit questions some other way other than email or voicemail
  7. Does the meeting website incorporate any of the following digital accessibility considerations (mark all that apply):
    • Text is structured with tags to guide screen readers and help provide more context and information about the content of a webpage.
    • Alternative text (alt text) is provided for images and other non-text content
    • Color is used to complement information, rather than convey it completely
    • Contrast is used between text and background
    • Visual focus indicators are included to give a user guidance or draw attention to important content
    • Information is not hidden behind animations that make it difficult for users to navigate and focus (examples: autoplay videos, chat windows, pop-up boxes, or oversized animated cursors)
    • Other
    • Unsure
    • None of the above

During the Meeting

All Meetings

  • What is the process or meeting structure like?
    • If there's a schedule, list it out
  • Can the public participate (Y/N)?
    • If so, how?
  • How is it known what agenda items can and cannot be commented on by the public?
  • If any, which agenda items don't allow any comment or response from the public?
